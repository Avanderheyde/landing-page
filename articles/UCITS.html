UCITS

Published: May 2025

Farmers markets encourage sustainable agriculture, offer fresh food, and connect communities. Nonetheless, they aren’t strictly necessary anymore. Historically, farmers markets solved a real coordination problem. Without them, if someone wanted meat, honey, and produce, they would have to travel to different locations. The invention of money made transacting for these products simpler, but not more convenient—everyone still needed to know when and where to trade. Farmers markets maximize liquidity by offering one location and limited hours. Today, that need is gone. Food can be purchased remotely or near constantly at grocery stores, demonstrating that farmers markets are a community event, not a commercial necessity.

Stock exchanges were built for the same reason: to coordinate trade. Designating a venue and market hours made sense when trading happened face-to-face. With electronic platforms and global connectivity, these time restrictions are increasingly artificial and costly. If I want an heirloom tomato on a Tuesday night, I’m out of luck. If I want to buy SPY at that same time, I’m still out of luck—and that shouldn't be the case. Through the example of UCITS, we demonstrate why traditional finance rails cannot easily solve this coordination problem.

Exchanges play a crucial role in modern markets. They develop trading infrastructure, ensure fair practices, innovate with order types, and maintain market integrity. Nonetheless, their rigid hours and geographic constraints limit access in ways that no longer match how global investors actually operate. We believe decentralized ledger technology can solve these challenges.

UCITS Primer

This article is not meant to be a deep dive into UCITS (examples are here, here, or here), but is intended to use them to demonstrate that liquidity can be sourced near constantly in modern markets, but trading boundaries lead to unnecessary costs. UCITS is like a designation assigned to a fund—frequently mutual funds and ETPs—to demonstrate that the fund meets certain provisions to trade across many exchanges. The designation was established initially in Europe since the continent is filled with local exchanges. Countries that approve UCITS have expanded to those as geographically distant as Mexico and Saudi Arabia, demonstrating that a broad range of currencies and time zones can support liquidity for one fund.

Fees

Compared to non-UCITS compliant securities, UCITS frequently have higher fees because of the regulatory complexity. For example, iShares issues both the US domiciled IVV and the Ireland domiciled CSPX, both of which track the S&P 500. IVV charges 3bps annually whereas CSPX charges 7bps. If UK traders want the product currency hedged (GSPX), then the expense ratio rises to 10bps. These funds all trade pretty much identically from a market making and investment perspective, but the lack of ability to net and differing expense ratios represent structural inefficiencies.

(For a quick aside in this shorter article, some funds like the US domiciled EWJ have a much higher expense ratio than their UCITS counterpart [50 vs 12 bps]. We ascribe this to first mover advantage and the availability of other MSCI UCITS like MXJP which also has a fee of 12bps.)

The baseline higher fees cover higher legal costs to ensure compliance, but the products trade pretty much identically across exchanges and hours. For investors who do not have access to US exchanges, these products are extremely valuable, though the relatively lower liquidity, regulatory hurdles, and higher fees obviously result in a product less desirable to trade than IVV.

Inefficiencies and Costs

UCITS have slightly more complicated regulatory challenges than non-UCITS compliant securities, making up a small portion of fees. Nonetheless, there are many inefficiencies that are related to traditional finance rails that lead to undue costs on consumers. Take this stylized example.

Market Maker A exists in the UK and quotes CSPX LN. During the day, they observe many inflows and needs to create new shares to flatten their short position. These shares are created at 4pm ET because that is when the NAV stamps. Because the markets close earlier in Europe than the US, they need to submit their creation far before US markets are closed (page 94). Market Maker B exists in the US and quotes IVV. They observe outflows and submit a redemption order. Because CSPX LN is not domiciled in the same location as IVV, the flows do not net so authorized participants receive double the fees, trading fees (exchange, regulatory body, etc.) are doubled, and market maker A (if they decided to continue trading after the cutoff) will have observed an extra day of holding costs if they observe the reversing flows.

If markets were unified and able to trade 24/7, such as on crypto exchanges, these fee reversals and varying cutoff times will obviated. Lack of trading transparency to regulators across markets makes these fees necessary, enriching intermediaries who connect rails across countries and facilitate creations/redemptions. By leveraging decentralized ledger technology, however, regulators will have more insight into trading behavior, resulting in lower fees and better products.

What can we learn from UCITS?

UCITS exist because of market inefficiencies. Not everyone has access to invest in all markets, creating structural challenges for capital formation and allocation. Some countries do not support the regulations required for UCITS listing, others do not have expansive exchanges. Regardless, investors hoping to trade ETPs that track the S&P 500, for instance, need to hope they are in the handful of countries that offer it. Those who are able to trade them have to pay for costly legal fees, cross-country interactions, and monopoly costs.

Since UCITS are traded globally and across market hours, we see the coordination problem solved by exchanges should be a thing of the past. Market makers quote UCITS in Singapore and Chile, demonstrating that liquidity and pricing can be counted on around the clock. By continuing with fragmented liquidity across individual exchanges, investors lose. Regulators historically could dismiss these concerns over transparency and listing requirements, but on decentralized exchanges, global regulators will have the chance to develop a unified framework that can help enable safe trading everywhere.

By creating a new class of ETPs on crypto rails, these trading problems can be diminished. Transparency of markets reduces the need for costly intermediaries and simplifies regulatory challenges. These exchanges can work to solve the same problems that they have worked on in the past: KYC/AML/compliance and improved trading experiences. Creating a new UCITS-like regulatory framework to trade across borders in these exchanges, markets will be a key unlock in building the market for ETPs, helping all parties involved in their trading except a few entrenched intermediaries.
